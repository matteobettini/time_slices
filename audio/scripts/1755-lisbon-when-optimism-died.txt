At nine-forty in the morning on All Saints' Day, seventeen fifty-five, the churches of Lisbon were packed.

It was the holiest day of the year. Candles burned on every altar. The faithful knelt in prayer. And then the ground began to move.

Three massive shocks brought down palaces, monasteries, the newly built opera house. Survivors stumbled through streets filling with rubble and fled toward the waterfront. There, they watched something impossible: the Tagus River draining away, revealing the muddy seabed littered with ancient wrecks. Forty minutes later, the first tsunami came.

Then the fires began — all those candles, all that devotion, turned to destruction. The blazes burned for days.

Estimates vary, but perhaps forty thousand people died that morning. Among the losses: Titian paintings in the royal palace, Vasco da Gama's original logs, centuries of archives — and something less tangible but equally important: Europe's faith that we live in a rational, benevolent cosmos.

Gottfried Leibniz had argued, decades earlier, that God — being perfect — must have created the best of all possible worlds. Alexander Pope had versified the idea: whatever is, is right. This optimism dominated European philosophy.

Then Lisbon fell.

Voltaire's response was fury. His Poem on the Lisbon Disaster attacked the optimists directly. Three years later, Candide gave the world Doctor Pangloss — the philosopher who insists, amid rape and murder and earthquake, that everything is for the best. It remains the most savage satire of theodicy ever written.

But others drew different conclusions. Jean-Jacques Rousseau argued that nature wasn't to blame — humans were, for building seven-storey buildings on unstable ground. This was the birth of disaster sociology. Meanwhile, in distant Königsberg, a young philosopher named Immanuel Kant published three essays explaining earthquakes through natural causes alone, rejecting any talk of divine punishment.

And the Marquis of Pombal, the king's chief minister, simply rebuilt. But he rebuilt differently: standardized grids, prefabricated modules, flexible wooden frames within the walls to absorb future shocks. Lisbon became the first earthquake-proof city — Enlightenment reason responding to Enlightenment crisis.

The earth doesn't care about our prayers. That was the lesson. What we do with that knowledge — that was the question seventeen fifty-five left us.
