The year is one thousand, and the world holds its breath.

In Rome, a young emperor barely twenty years old rules from a palace on the Aventine Hill. Otto the Third dreams of something audacious: a Renovatio Imperii Romanorum — a renewal of the Roman Empire itself. And sitting in Peter's chair, advising him, is his former tutor — a man named Gerbert, now Pope Sylvester the Second.

The name is no accident. Sylvester the First baptized Constantine. Sylvester the Second would crown this new Constantine's vision.

Gerbert is perhaps the most learned man in Christendom. He studied in Muslim Spain, at Vic and Ripoll, where he discovered Hindu-Arabic numerals and the abacus. He builds astronomical instruments — armillary spheres, celestial globes — that let students visualise the heavens. His curriculum resurrects the seven liberal arts, dormant since Boethius.

His contemporaries whisper that he learned sorcery from the Arabs. They cannot believe mere study could produce such knowledge.

In March, Otto journeys north to Gniezno in Poland, praying at the tomb of the martyred Adalbert. There he grants the Polish Church its own archbishopric, free from German control. At Christmas, Stephen the First receives a crown from Sylvester's hands, founding Christian Hungary. Christendom is expanding — and consolidating.

Meanwhile, at the monastery of Reichenau on Lake Constance, monks are creating the most magnificent manuscripts of the medieval West. The Gospels of Otto the Third shimmer with gold leaf and Byzantine grandeur. They show the emperor enthroned, receiving homage from the personifications of Roma, Gallia, Germania, and Sclavinia. Theology made visible. Sacred and secular power reflecting one another.

Across France, bishops and abbots gather for Peace of God councils, binding feudal warriors with oaths sworn on relics. Violence must yield to order.

A generation later, the monk Raoul Glaber will write that around the millennium, the whole world shook off the dust of ages and clothed itself in a white mantle of churches. Not panic — but renewal. Stone basilicas rising where wooden churches once stood.

The year one thousand. Not the end of the world. The beginning of Europe.
