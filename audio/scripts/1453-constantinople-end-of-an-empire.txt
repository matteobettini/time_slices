Fourteen fifty-three. The year empires die.

On the morning of May twenty-ninth, after fifty-three days of siege, the unthinkable happens. The Theodosian walls — defenses that held for a thousand years, that turned back Huns and Persians and Arabs — finally break. Mehmed the Second, just twenty-one years old, sends his Ottoman forces pouring through the breach. Constantine the Eleventh, last emperor of Rome, tears off his imperial regalia and charges into the fighting. His body is never found.

By nightfall, the city that Constantine the Great consecrated in three-thirty CE belongs to Islam.

But this is not simply a military catastrophe. It is a scattering.

The scholars flee. Cardinal Bessarion escapes with five hundred Greek manuscripts — Plato's dialogues, Aristotle's complete works, texts the West had nearly forgotten. He carries them to Venice, seeds them across Italy. Within a generation, these books will transform how Europe thinks.

And in the immediate aftermath of the disaster, a German philosopher named Nicholas of Cusa writes something unprecedented. His De Pace Fidei — On the Peace of Faith — imagines a heavenly council where representatives of all the world's religions gather to seek common ground. Not through crusade. Through reason. One religion in a variety of rites. It is perhaps the first Western philosophical argument for religious tolerance, written in the very shadow of Christian defeat.

The same year, in France, the Battle of Castillon ends the Hundred Years' War — the first major European battle won by field artillery. John Talbot, the legendary English commander, is killed when a cannonball strikes his horse.

And in Mainz, Johannes Gutenberg is printing Bibles.

Gunpowder and movable type. The old certainties falling to both.

Constantinople's civilization does not die. It disperses. Greek artists flee to Crete and found a school that will produce El Greco. Greek texts flood Italian libraries and fuel the Renaissance. Greek philosophy arrives just as printing can amplify it.

The city falls. But what it carried — the preserved knowledge of the ancient world — scatters like seeds across Europe.

Fourteen fifty-three. The end. And the beginning.
