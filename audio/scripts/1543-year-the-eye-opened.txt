Fifteen forty-three. In Nuremberg, a printer sets the final page of a book that will shake the heavens. In Basel, another printer binds a folio that will crack open the human body. Two revolutions, arriving within months of each other.

Nicolaus Copernicus, a Polish canon, has spent thirty years perfecting his argument. The sun does not circle the earth. We circle the sun. The mathematics are elegant, the implications terrifying. Copernicus dedicates his book to Pope Paul the Third, hoping for protection. He need not have worried — he will die in May, reportedly holding the first printed copy on his deathbed.

But something strange happens. A Lutheran theologian named Osiander, supervising the printing, slips an unauthorized preface into the book. It calls heliocentrism a mere calculating device, not physical reality. Copernicus never approved this. For decades, readers will think the author himself was hedging.

Meanwhile, in Basel, a young Flemish anatomist named Andreas Vesalius is revolutionizing medicine. At twenty-eight, he publishes the Fabrica — seven books on the structure of the human body, illustrated with over two hundred woodcuts from Titian's workshop.

For thirteen hundred years, physicians had read Galen aloud during dissections while barber-surgeons cut. Vesalius changes the method entirely: the physician must cut with his own hands and look with his own eyes. He finds Galen riddled with errors — the great Greek physician had dissected apes, not humans.

The Fabrica's illustrations are stunning. Dissected bodies stand in classical poses against Italian landscapes, their muscles exposed, their bones revealed. Art serves science. Beauty illuminates truth.

Both books share something deeper than a publication year. They share a method. Vesalius corrects Galen by cutting bodies. Copernicus corrects Ptolemy by calculating orbits. Both replace medieval deference to ancient texts with a new confidence: we can see for ourselves.

The printing press multiplies both texts across Europe. Galileo will read Copernicus. Harvey will read Vesalius. Ideas travel faster than inquisitors.

The Church will ban Copernicus's book in sixteen sixteen — seventy-three years too late. By then, the eye has opened. Body and cosmos, cut apart in the same year, will never be sealed shut again.
