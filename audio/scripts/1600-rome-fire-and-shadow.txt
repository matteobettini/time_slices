February seventeenth, sixteen hundred. The dawn breaks cold over Rome's Campo de' Fiori.

A crowd has gathered — not for the market that usually fills this square, but for an execution. At its centre stands Giordano Bruno, philosopher, heretic, dreamer of infinite worlds. He has been in the Inquisition's dungeons for eight years. He has refused, again and again, to recant.

They have gagged him. The authorities fear his final words more than his writings. Bruno claimed the universe was infinite — that the stars were distant suns, each with their own worlds, perhaps their own life. He believed God was not separate from creation but pervaded it entirely, an infinite divinity requiring an infinite cosmos. To propose a finite universe, he argued, was to insult the infinite God.

The flames rise. Bruno burns.

Across Europe, his death sends tremors through the intellectual world. In Padua, a young mathematician named Galileo takes note — he will be more careful than Bruno, though not careful enough. In London, William Shakespeare is writing his greatest tragedy. "To be or not to be," Hamlet will ask, grappling with the same cosmic uncertainty that Bruno faced.

And in Rome itself, a painter named Caravaggio is revolutionising art. His figures emerge from profound darkness into shafts of uncertain light — saints painted with dirty feet, angels with peasant faces. He's creating the visual language of an age that no longer trusts easy answers.

In the same year, William Gilbert publishes De Magnete, proving through careful experiment that the Earth is a giant magnet — invisible forces governing the planet itself. Queen Elizabeth grants a charter to the East India Company. The old world is ending; a new one is being born.

Bruno's pyre marks a turning point. The medieval cosmos — finite, hierarchical, with humanity at its centre — is dissolving. What remains is shadow and inquiry, doubt and the terrible freedom of an open universe.

The flames have died. The question remains: what lies beyond?
