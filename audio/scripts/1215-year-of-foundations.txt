The year twelve fifteen. A single year when the medieval world laid foundations that would shape centuries.

In England, at a meadow called Runnymede by the Thames, rebellious barons surround King John. He has taxed them to ruin, lost their lands in France, made enemies of every power in his realm. Now they force him to seal a document: the Magna Carta. Sixty-three clauses declaring that even a king is bound by law. Pope Innocent the Third will annul it within weeks. Civil war will follow. But something has cracked. The idea that no ruler stands above the law — once written, cannot be unwritten.

That same November, in Rome, Pope Innocent convenes the Fourth Lateran Council. Over four hundred bishops. Eight hundred abbots. Envoys from every Christian kingdom. The crowd is so vast that the Pope himself struggles to enter Saint Peter's for Mass. They issue seventy-one decrees that reshape medieval life. Annual confession becomes mandatory. Jews and Muslims must wear distinctive clothing. A new crusade is proclaimed.

And here's the paradox: the Council formally defines transubstantiation — the doctrine that bread and wine become Christ's body and blood — using Aristotelian language of substance and accidents. Yet at the University of Paris, that same pope has banned the study of Aristotle's natural philosophy. The Church uses philosophical categories while forbidding the philosophy.

Meanwhile, at Chartres, craftsmen are installing stained glass windows of such luminous blue that later generations will forget how to make them. Flying buttresses carry the weight outward, transforming walls into frames for light. The Gothic cathedral becomes a theological statement in stone: the church as heavenly Jerusalem.

In the German lands, Wolfram von Eschenbach has just completed Parzival — the story of a fool who becomes the Grail King. Gottfried von Strassburg dies leaving Tristan unfinished, his sympathetic treatment of adulterous love too controversial for easy resolution.

Francis of Assisi and Dominic de Guzman both attend the Council, seeking approval for their new orders. The mendicant friars will transform the Church — and the Dominicans will eventually reconcile Aristotle with Christian doctrine, despite the Paris ban.

The sun-and-moon theory. The king bound by law. Substance and accidents. Stone dissolving into light.

Twelve fifteen. The foundations of the medieval synthesis — and the cracks already forming within them.
