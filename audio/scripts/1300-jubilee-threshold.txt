The year thirteen hundred. Rome has declared something it has never declared before.

On February twenty-second, Pope Boniface the Eighth issues a papal bull announcing the first Christian Jubilee. Every pilgrim who visits Saint Peter's and Saint Paul's basilicas will receive complete forgiveness of sins. The response is overwhelming. Two hundred thousand people flood the Eternal City throughout the year — peasants and nobles, merchants and monks, all seeking the same promise: a clean slate before God.

Boniface is at the height of his power. He wears imperial regalia. He reportedly declares: I am Caesar, I am Emperor! The fresco painter Giotto captures his triumph on the loggia of Saint John Lateran, the Pope blessing the endless crowds below.

But Rome is not the only center of action this year.

In Florence, one of Europe's most prosperous cities, a thirty-five year old poet named Dante Alighieri holds political office. From June to August, he serves as one of the city's six Priors — its highest magistrates. He makes enemies. He exiles troublemakers from both warring factions. He doesn't know it yet, but these decisions will cost him everything.

Dante will never write the Divine Comedy in Florence. Within two years, his enemies will condemn him to exile. He'll wander Italy for the rest of his life. But when he finally writes his masterpiece, he'll set its action here, in this exact year — the pilgrim's journey through Hell beginning in Holy Week of thirteen hundred, the Jubilee Year itself.

Meanwhile, in Oxford, a Scottish Franciscan named John Duns Scotus is lecturing on philosophy. His students call him the Subtle Doctor. His arguments are intricate, precise, almost impossible to follow. But one idea will outlast the rest: his doctrine that the word 'being' means the same thing whether applied to God or creatures. It sounds technical. It isn't. Scotus is cracking the foundation of the previous century's great synthesis. Aquinas said God and creation are analogous. Scotus says they share the same fundamental nature.

The cracks are appearing everywhere.

Boniface will be dead within four years, humiliated by French agents at Anagni. The great banking families of Florence — the Bardi, the Peruzzi — will collapse forty-five years later. The Black Death will arrive in thirteen forty-eight and kill perhaps half of Europe.

But none of them know this yet.

In Rome, the pilgrims keep arriving. In Florence, Dante keeps making enemies. In Oxford, Scotus keeps drawing distinctions.

Thirteen hundred. The hinge year. Medieval Christendom at maximum articulation — just before it begins to crack.
