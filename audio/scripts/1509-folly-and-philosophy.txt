Fifteen oh nine. In Rome, a young painter from Urbino climbs a scaffold in the Vatican. In London, a Dutch scholar sits by the fire in a friend's house, laughing as he writes. They have never met. But together, they will define what the Renaissance means.

Raphael is twenty-six years old, and Pope Julius the Second has given him an impossible commission: decorate the papal apartments with frescoes that rival anything in Rome. He begins in the Stanza della Segnatura — the Pope's private study. On one wall, he paints theology. On another, poetry. And on the third, he paints philosophy itself.

The School of Athens. Plato and Aristotle walk through an idealized classical building, surrounded by every great thinker of antiquity. Pythagoras scribbles in a notebook. Euclid demonstrates geometry. Diogenes lounges on the steps like a vagrant. But look closer, and you see something extraordinary. Plato has the face of Leonardo da Vinci. Euclid looks exactly like Bramante, the architect building the new Saint Peter's. And brooding alone, writing on a block of marble, sits Michelangelo — or rather, Heraclitus with Michelangelo's features.

Raphael has hidden the living artists of Rome among the dead philosophers of Athens. The message is revolutionary: we are their equals. The Renaissance has achieved what it always promised — the rebirth of ancient wisdom.

But six hundred miles north, in Thomas More's house in London, Erasmus is writing a very different kind of philosophy. The Praise of Folly. Speaking through the voice of Folly herself, he mocks everything Rome takes seriously. Scholastic philosophers arguing about angels on pinheads. Monks who can't read but claim to speak for God. Popes who lead armies instead of souls.

The title itself is a joke — Moriae Encomium in Greek, which sounds like "praise of More." Erasmus writes it in about a week, recovering from kidney stones, laughing through the pain. It will become the bestselling book of the sixteenth century after the Bible.

Here is the paradox of fifteen oh nine. Raphael paints philosophy as harmony — ancient wisdom recovered, reason and faith reconciled. Erasmus writes philosophy as folly — the same tradition turned to satire. Both are humanists. Both believe in learning, in Greek, in going back to the sources. But one offers synthesis, the other mockery.

Pope Julius the Second connects them without knowing it. He is paying for The School of Athens. And he is exactly the corrupt warrior-pope that Erasmus mocks.

In London, John Colet founds Saint Paul's School — the first in England to teach Greek. In Westminster, Henry the Eighth takes the throne, a young Renaissance prince who plays music, writes poetry, and will one day tear the English church apart.

The humanists of fifteen oh nine do not know what is coming. Eight years later, Martin Luther will nail his theses to a door, and the world Raphael painted — ancient wisdom reborn in Christian harmony — will shatter forever.

But for one moment, in fifteen oh nine, it almost seemed possible. Philosophy as art. Folly as wisdom. The Renaissance at its strange, paradoxical height.
