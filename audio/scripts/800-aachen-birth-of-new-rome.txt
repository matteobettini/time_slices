Christmas Day, eight hundred. In Saint Peter's Basilica, a Frankish king kneels to pray.

Pope Leo the Third steps forward and places a golden crown on his head. The gathered Romans roar their acclamation: Emperor of the Romans! Charlemagne, the records say, was surprised. Perhaps. But nothing about this moment is accidental.

Three centuries have passed since the Western Roman Empire fell. For three hundred years, the idea of Rome has been a memory preserved only in books, in ruins, in the claims of Byzantine emperors far away in Constantinople. Now a new claimant has arrived.

But Charlemagne doesn't simply claim Rome. He builds a new one.

In Aachen, deep in Frankish territory, an extraordinary chapel rises. Its architect, Odo of Metz, has studied Byzantine churches, especially San Vitale in Ravenna. The result is an octagonal masterpiece, its dome soaring above columns plundered from Rome and Ravenna themselves. The message is unmistakable: we are the heirs. We are equal to Constantinople.

Yet Charlemagne knows that buildings alone cannot sustain an empire. What he needs is something subtler: the preservation and transmission of knowledge.

Enter Alcuin of York. This English scholar becomes the emperor's chief advisor on education. Together they establish schools throughout the empire. They standardize Latin grammar. And Alcuin's scribes develop a new script — Carolingian minuscule — so clear, so elegant, that it will become the foundation of the letters you're reading today.

In monasteries across Europe, monks copy ancient texts with new urgency. Many classical works survive only because Carolingian scribes preserved them. The chain of transmission, nearly broken, holds.

Charlemagne himself never learns to write. He keeps tablets under his pillow, practicing his letters, but never masters them. The man who saves Western literacy cannot produce it himself.

In Constantinople, Empress Irene watches. Negotiations for a marriage between the two rulers raise the possibility of reuniting the Roman Empire, East and West, under one ruling pair. It never happens. Irene is deposed. Two Romes will face each other across Europe for centuries.

But in Aachen, the work continues. The minuscule script. The copied manuscripts. The schools where logic and grammar and rhetoric are taught alongside theology.

A new Rome is being born. Not in marble, but in parchment.
