Nineteen sixty-eight. The year the world catches fire.

It begins with a lunar new year offensive that shatters American certainties. In January, Viet Cong fighters storm the U.S. Embassy compound in Saigon. The images — played on every television in America — expose the lie. Victory was never around the corner.

In April, Martin Luther King Jr. is shot on a Memphis hotel balcony. Robert Kennedy breaks the news in Indianapolis, quoting Greek tragedy: wisdom through suffering. Cities across America burn. Two months later, Kennedy himself is dead — shot in a Los Angeles hotel kitchen.

But nineteen sixty-eight is not only American tragedy. In Prague, Alexander Dubček promises socialism with a human face. For one brilliant spring, Czechoslovakia loosens censorship, permits debate, dreams of freedom within the socialist system. In August, Soviet tanks roll in. The Prague Spring dies beneath their treads.

And in Paris — Paris explodes.

What starts as student protests at Nanterre spreads to the Sorbonne. By mid-May, ten million French workers are on strike. Barricades rise in the Latin Quarter. Posters appear: "Sous les pavés, la plage!" Under the cobblestones, the beach. De Gaulle flees briefly to Germany; it seems, for one electric moment, that the Fifth Republic might fall.

Behind the barricades, ideas circulate. Herbert Marcuse's analysis of one-dimensional society. Guy Debord's thesis that modern life has become spectacle — representation consuming reality. Jacques Derrida quietly dismantling the very structures the students are loudly rejecting.

In art, new movements crystallize. Conceptual artists declare the idea is the work — execution optional. Land artists drag sculpture into the Nevada desert. Andy Warhol is shot in June and survives; the Pope of Pop retreats from filmmaking.

Meanwhile, Kubrick's monolith stands in cinemas — enigmatic, alien, pointing toward something that cannot be named.

What connects Prague to Paris to Memphis to Saigon? The postwar consensus cracking. A global generation, raised on television, seeing the same images, reading the same books, recognizing — across borders and languages — the same enemy: structures that pretend to be nature.

The old authorities lose their spell. Not in one place. Everywhere. All at once.

Nineteen sixty-eight. The hinge. The fracture. The year the fire spreads.
