Eighteen forty-eight. The Year of Revolutions. The Springtime of Peoples. The moment when the old order finally cracked.

On February twenty-first, in a London print shop, a twenty-three page pamphlet appeared. "A specter is haunting Europe," it began, "the specter of communism." Karl Marx and Friedrich Engels had finished writing the Communist Manifesto just weeks earlier. Three days later — purely by coincidence — Paris erupted.

King Louis Philippe had ruled France for eighteen years. The Citizen King, they called him, with his bourgeois umbrella and his pretense of constitutional monarchy. But when he banned political gatherings, the people took to the streets. By February twenty-fourth, the king had abdicated and fled to England. The Second Republic was proclaimed.

The shockwave traveled faster than any news could. In Vienna, students and workers stormed the streets on March thirteenth, demanding the head of Prince Metternich — the architect of post-Napoleonic reaction who had kept Europe frozen since eighteen fifteen. By evening, Metternich was gone, scrambling toward exile in London. The Austrian Empire began to fracture: Hungary, Bohemia, the Italian states — all demanding freedom, constitutions, nationhood.

Revolution swept the German states, the Italian peninsula, from Sicily to Prague. Thrones tottered. Aristocrats trembled. For a brief, brilliant moment, it seemed the old feudal order might simply dissolve.

Meanwhile, in a London studio, seven young artists were planning their own revolution. William Holman Hunt, John Everett Millais, Dante Gabriel Rossetti — they called themselves the Pre-Raphaelite Brotherhood. Their enemy wasn't a king but an academy: the Royal Academy's stale conventions, descended from Raphael, that had drained art of sincerity. They wanted truth to nature, luminous color, medieval honesty.

In Paris, a young painter named Gustave Courbet exhibited seven paintings at a Salon without a jury — the revolution had abolished it. His After Dinner at Ornans showed ordinary peasants at an ordinary meal, painted on a scale reserved for history and myth. Realism was being born.

And in Yorkshire, Emily Brontë was dying. She had published Wuthering Heights just the year before — wild, Gothic, uncompromising. On December nineteenth, she refused medical help until the end. Consumption took her at thirty.

By winter, most revolutions had failed. Troops restored order. Kings returned to thrones. But serfdom was abolished in Austria. And something had changed permanently: the idea that peoples could remake their own states.

The Manifesto's most famous line says that all history is the history of class struggle. The revolutions of eighteen forty-eight proved it — and proved that the struggle was far from over.
